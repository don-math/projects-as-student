Introduction

This research is about to predict a worker’s wage based on other information, such as health, education, age. After building the model based the four most important predictors, I further investigated the variance of the coefficients of the linear model and explored the possibility of using Ridge and Lasso regressions.

Data

Mid-Atlantic Wage Data: Wage and other data for a group of 3000 male workers in the Mid-Atlantic region.

Format

A data frame with 3000 observations on the following 11 variables.

year: Year that wage information was recorded

age: Age of worker

maritl: A factor with levels 1. Never Married 2. Married 3. Widowed 4. Divorced and 5. Separated indicating marital status

race: A factor with levels 1. White 2. Black 3. Asian and 4. Other indicating race

education: A factor with levels 1. < HS Grad 2. HS Grad 3. Some College 4. College Grad and 5. Advanced Degree indicating education level

region: Region of the country (mid-atlantic only)

jobclass: A factor with levels 1. Industrial and 2. Information indicating type of job

health: A factor with levels 1. <=Good and 2. >=Very Good indicating health level of worker

health_ins: A factor with levels 1. Yes and 2. No indicating whether worker has health insurance

logwage: Log of workers wage

wage: Workers raw wage

Model building

Variables selection

I am using the logwage as a response for the nature of inequality of earnings among different members in the society. That is the distribution of wages usually has a long tail in capitalism societies, i.e. there are always people like Bill Gates who have exponentially high incomes. If we do not take the log, these people can be outliers and negatively influence the prediction of our linear models. Initially, I tried to fit model directly on wages which gives me very high errors. After using the logwage, I have found the errors are generally limited to 10% of actual values which is satisfactory.

We want to use as less predictors as necessary in the model, so we plot both R2R2 and adjusted R2R2. The R2R2 is a measure of how much more variation can be explained by the fitted model compared the null model which is just predicting every member by using average of the group. While adjusted R2R2 is the $R2 adjusted with the number of predictors. The R2R2 is always going up as we add more predictors, no matter they are really effective in the model or not. But the adjusted R2R2 will go up only when the added predictor is efficient enough in improving the prediction result.\

Looking the plots of two values, they are always going up as the number of predictors go up, indicating in this case adding more predictors are always helpful in improving the predictio results.

plot(sum.best.subset$rsq, pch=2, xlab = "# of Predictors", ylab = "R^2 (black) and Adjusted R^2 (red)")
points(sum.best.subset$adjr2, pch=3, col="red")
abline(h=max(sum.best.subset$rsq), lty=3)
abline(h=max(sum.best.subset$adjr2), lty=3, col="red")

CpCp is an estimate of test MSE. It has a component of number of predictors. Only adding effective predictors will help reduce test errors. As you can see from the below graph, the CpCp is always decreaing, which again implies adding all the predictors.

plot(sum.best.subset$cp, pch=1, xlab="# of Predictors", ylab="C_p")

The colinearity problem means certain predictors are (nearly) linearly dependent with each other. It may cause the standard errors of the estimate increase significantly. And Variance inflation factor (VIF) is a measure of colinearity among predictors. If the VIF value is greater than 5 or 10, it typically indicates the problem of colinearity. Here all of our VIF values are smaller than 2, which is great sign that we are free of this colinearity problem.

The leave one out cross-validation is a method to estimate the test error. Each time the algorithm fits on all except for one of the data and test the remained one. The remained one works as the test set, which can reveal some information about our test errors. Here we have 0.085997 indicating that we should expect that the mean test error to be 0.086, which is very small compared with the range of logwage (3 - 5).

The bootstrap is a method to estimate the variation of the coefficients of our model. It randomly samples from the original data set, fit the model and record the coefficients. This process repeat to a designated times (here is 1000 times). Then we can summarize the variation information about the coefficients, such as the standard errors.\

Below the t1 means the intercept of our model and it has an bias-0.002 and standard error of 0.032. t2 corresponds to the coefficient of age, etc. All of the standard errors here are small, indicating our model has a small variance.

# Use Bootstrap to estimate the uncertainty coefficients of the model
boot.fn=function (data ,index)
return(coef(glm(logwage ~ age + edu + hea + maritl + race + jobclass,data=d , subset=index)))
boot(d ,boot.fn ,1000)

Ridge and Lasso regressions

The Ridge regression adds a constraint to residual sum square by using a λλ times the sum sequares of the coefficients As we can see from the below graph as λλ increases the coefficients gradually approaches to zero.

x=model.matrix(logwage∼age + edu + hea + maritl + race + jobclass,d )[,-1]
y=Wage$logwage
grid=10^seq(10,-2, length =100)
ridge.mod=glmnet (x,y,alpha=0, lambda=grid)
plot(ridge.mod)

Here I am separating the data into test and train sets.

set.seed(1)
train=sample (1: nrow(x), nrow(x)/2)
test=(-train)
y.test=y[test]

Here I use the train set to choose the smallest λλ 0.015525.The below graph shows the mean squared error and logλlogλ.

set.seed(1)
cv.out=cv.glmnet(x[train ,],y[ train],alpha=0)
plot(cv.out)

bestlam =cv.out$lambda.min
bestlam
## [1] 0.01552464

Using the best λλ we get above, we estimate the mean squared error on the test set and get 0.085 which is small compared with logwage’s range.

ridge.pred=predict (ridge.mod ,s=bestlam ,newx=x[test ,])
mean((ridge.pred -y.test)^2)
## [1] 0.08358034

Similar with ridge, the lasso regression add a constraint to the regression by adding a λλ times the sum of the absolute values of the coefficients. But different from the ridge, the lasso will shink the coefficients to zero much faster.As we can see from the below graph, many of the coefficients (denote by lines of different colors) hits zero quickly.

lasso.mod=glmnet(x[train ,],y[ train],alpha=1, lambda =grid)
